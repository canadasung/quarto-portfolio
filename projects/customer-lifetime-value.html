Overview

Customer Lifetime Value (CLV), also commonly known as LTV, estimates the total net profit a business can expect from a customer over the duration of their relationship. This presentation covers why CLV matters, the major families of CLV calculation methods, and worked examples for each.

Definition of a Cohort

Groups customers by acquisition period or channel (e.g., signup month or marketing channel) and tracks their cumulative revenue over time. The way of grouping can vary based on the business context and the insights sought.

Why CLV is hard

There is no single “correct” way to calculate CLV — every method involves assumptions and trade-offs.

  • Even well-built models typically carry 15–25% error margins.
  • CLV is usually applied to 3–7 year forecasting horizons, so small errors compound.
  • Every approach faces the same dilemma: a CLV model construction can be at most two of Simple, Accurate, and Inexpensive — never all three.

Historical CLV

Historical methods look backward at actual customer revenue/profit data.

ARPU / ARPA method

Average Revenue Per User (ARPU) or Average Revenue Per Account (ARPA) multiplies average revenue per customer by the expected customer lifespan.

Worked example — Alice vs. Bob

Alice: (150 + 50 + 100) / 6 months = $50 ARPU, 6 months lifespan → CLV = $300

Bob: (45 + 75 +100) / 2 months = $110 ARPU, 2 months lifespan → CLV = $220

(50 + 110) / 2 = $80 per customer

12 months value: $80 * 12 months = $960

24 months value: $80 * 24 months = $1,920

  • Alice spends less per month but stays subscribed much longer than Bob.
  • Even though Bob’s monthly spend is higher, Alice’s longer retention can make her CLV greater — illustrating why retention matters as much as spend.

Key points of Historical CLV methods

There are some factors that may not be captured by historical methods:

  • Different customers have different behavior patterns that may not be reflected in averages. Use cohort method to narrow down to more specific segments.
  • Future changes in customer behavior or market conditions may not be captured, such as internally changed by offerings or externally changed by market trends.
  • Not enough data when a business is too young or data duration is too short to capture the full customer lifecycle.

Predictive CLV

Predictive methods use statistical models to forecast future value rather than relying solely on historical averages.

Key Variables

Four variables drive most predictive CLV calculations:

  • Acquisition Cost / Retention Cost:

The cost to acquire a new customer or retain an existing one (e.g., marketing spend per conversion).

  • Net Profit Margin:

    • Among the cohorts, the share of revenue retained as profit after costs.

    • Profit Margin Ratio: Net Income / Net Sales

    • Usually the Finance team can provide this number, but if not, general rules are:

      • 10% for public companies
      • 15% for private companies that are scaling predictably
      • 20%-25% for private companies that have not yet reached scale and predictable growth

  • Retention / Churn Rate and Expected Lifespan:

\[ \text{Retention Rate} = \frac{\text{Customers at End of Period} - \text{New Customers Acquired}}{\text{Customers at Start of Period}} \]

The Churn Rate is the complement of the Retention Rate: \[ \text{Churn Rate} = 1 - \text{Retention Rate} \]

We can use the retention rate to estimate the expected customer lifespan: \[ \text{Expected Customer Lifespan} = \frac{1}{\text{Churn Rate}} \]

  • Discount Rate:

Accounts for the time value of money when projecting future profits to present value. Get this from the Finance team, or use 1 as a starting point for simplicity.

Formula

After getting the key variables, we can plug them into the formula to calculate CLV:

Simple formula

\[ CLV = \text{Average Revenue per Customer} \times \text{Expected Customer Lifespan} \]

Complete formula

A more complete formula incorporates profit margins, retention, and the time value of money:

\[ CLV = \left( \frac{\text{Revenue per Customer} \times \text{Gross Margin}}{1 - \text{Retention Rate} + \text{Discount Rate}} \right) - \text{Acquisition Cost} \]

Conclusion

CLV ultimately helps answer three fundamental business questions:

  1. How much should we spend to acquire a customer?
  2. Which customer segments are worth investing in retaining?
  3. How sustainable is our current growth strategy?

Choosing a CLV method is less about finding the “perfect” formula and more about matching the right balance of simplicity, accuracy, and cost to the decision at hand.
