Overview

Market Basket Analysis (MBA) — also known as affinity analysis — is a data mining technique based on conditional probability (Bayes’ Theorem) used to discover co-occurrence relationships among products (the classic “beers and diapers” example). It is widely adopted across retail, e-commerce, and finance for cross-sell/upsell strategy, bundling and discounting, shelf or page placement, and fraud detection.

Why Market Basket Analysis

  • Easy to understand and implement: gather transactional data with a defined “basket” (e.g., by customer or by transaction).
  • Widely applicable: cross-sell/upsell, bundle/discount strategy, shelf or webpage display optimization, and fraud pattern detection in finance.

Analytic Terms

Occurrence & Support

Occurrence / Co-occurrence: the number of times an item (or set of items) is purchased within the group.

Support: the frequency (ratio) of an occurrence within the group.

Worked example — out of 100 customers:

  • Bread is purchased by 50 customers → Support(Bread) = 50/100 = 0.5
  • Butter is purchased by 30 customers → Support(Butter) = 30/100 = 0.3
  • Bread and Butter together are purchased 20 times → Support(Co-occurrence) = 20/100 = 0.2

Confidence

Confidence: the probability of both items being purchased, given that one item was purchased.

\[ Confidence(Bread) = \frac{Support(Co\text{-}occur)}{Support(Bread)} = \frac{0.2}{0.5} = 0.4 \]

\[ Confidence(Butter) = \frac{Support(Co\text{-}occur)}{Support(Butter)} = \frac{0.2}{0.3} = 0.6667 \]

Lift

Lift: the key indicator — the likelihood that two products go together. Only meaningful when greater than 1 (the higher, the stronger the association), though there is no universal market standard for “high enough”; values are interpreted relative to other outputs.

\[ Lift(Bread \,\&\, Butter) = \frac{Support(Co\text{-}occur)}{Support(Bread) \times Support(Butter)} = \frac{0.2}{0.15} = 1.33 \]

Benefits of MBA

  • Accurate and easy to read: Support, Confidence, and Lift are simple, interpretable metrics.
  • Automation is possible: all products analyzed in under 10 minutes, versus manual correlation analysis comparing only 3–4 products per day.
  • Balances data cleanliness vs. time cost: different levels of data cleanliness still reveal similar patterns.
  • Additional insights: product popularity, and comparisons across years, seasons, or stores.

Constraints

  • Similar product formats are still compared: e.g., 1L milk vs. 2L milk. Mitigate by using filtering features to screen products.
  • Basic MBA is limited to 1-to-1 relationships: e.g., product A and product B. Advanced MBA (A + B + C → D) requires more advanced software/services.
  • Data cleaning and model maintenance:
    • Data training is required.
    • Raw data needs cleaning to reduce model run time.
    • Procedures must be modified or re-created if the analysis goals change (e.g., changes to the basket or product hierarchy).

Conclusion — SMART in Creating MBA Analytics

  • Specific: can be measured under different product hierarchies.
  • Measurable: Support, Confidence, and Lift indicators support decision-making.
  • Achievable: cost-effective when there’s no budget for external software licenses for the functionality and require self-build.
  • Relevant: directly matches cross-selling strategy goals.
  • Timeliness: automation enables fast, easy-to-learn results.
